Finnish writer, former building construction supervisor, whose books were national bestsellers every autumn when they appeared before the Father's Day. P‰‰talo's major work, the nearly 17 000 pages long autobiographical Iijoki series, depicts the author's own path from the backwoods of Northeast Finland to the publication of his first book and to literary fame. The last part of the series, Pˆlhˆkanto Iijoen tˆrm‰ss‰ (1998), finished his 28-year long writing job. P‰‰talo died two years later.

Kalle P‰‰talo was born in Taivalkoski in the small village of Jokij‰rvi. His father, Lauri Herman P‰‰talo, called Herkko, was a lumberjack, hard working, stingy, temperament man who suffered from mental problems. He married in 1913 Priitta-Stiina (Neulikko) P‰‰talo, called Riitu, who was seven years younger. Kalle admired and feared his father who prided himself on doing his work better that anybody in the region, but who also mastered an ingeniously colorful language which he used to humiliate his family.

The family lived in other people's rooms before Herkko managed to save enough money to build a humble house on the shore of the river Jokij‰rvi. It was to become the center of Kalle's life, his novels and later a tourist attraction. Kalle's childhood was poor and full of hard labour, but already at the early age he dreamed to be writer. He knew the letters and could writer his name before entering the elementary school. P‰‰talo read widely, lending books from the school library and the public library which was situated in the centre of the county, 20 kilometers from his home. Among his favorite books were Robinson Crusoe, Robin Hood, J‰‰meren sankari by Kaarlo H‰nninen and Runeberg's epic poem V‰nrikki Stoolin tarinat.

"Yritin kirjoittaa keskitalven vitilumella v‰lpp‰‰vist‰ auringon s‰desiruista, kev‰‰n hankikannosta, juoksemaan auenneesta kev‰tpurosta, muuttolintujen saapumisesta ja l‰htemisest‰, jalan alla murenevasta j‰k‰l‰st‰, suon- ja kanervien tuoksusta - kaikista niist‰ luonnonilmiˆist‰, jotka olivat minussa her‰tt‰neet voimakkaita tunnetiloja. As a student P‰‰talo was above average, better than his admits in his Iijoki series, although he was jailed for a short time for playing card. From 1933 to 1939 P‰‰talo was a forest worker, but managed to find time in the hard conditions for creative writing. He read several times Mika Waltari's guide for an aspiring writer, Aiotko kirjailijaksi (1935). It was not until 1975 when P‰‰talo met the older writer. Waltari was deeply moved when P‰‰talo showed him his well-worn copy of the book. During The Winter War (1939-40) and Continuation War (1939-1944) he served at the Finnish Army, and was wounded in the front. These years he later described in such novels as Ukkosen ‰‰ni (1979) and Tuulessa ja tuskussa (1981). P‰‰talo's viewpoint to the war arises from tightly restricted individual experiences, and as V‰inˆ Linna in The Unknown Soldier (1954), he concentrated on the ordinary soldiers.

After the war P‰‰talo married and moved to Tampere where he studied at the technical school and become a building construction supervisor in 1949. His short stories started to appear in the magazines Pohjolan J‰tk‰, Sirpale, Nyyrikki, and Perjantai. In 1951-52 P‰‰talo worked in Taivalkoski.  His first marriage ended in divorce and he the married in 1955 Elli Helena (Janakka) P‰‰talo; they had two daughters.

P‰‰talo's childhood's dream came finally true at the age of 39 when his first novel Ihmisi‰ telineill‰ came out. From the 1960s P‰‰talo devoted himself entirely to writing. Koillismaa (1960, Our Daily Bread), a story about the struggle to survive during the Great Depression in Finland, was translated into English by Richard Impola in 1990 and Myrsky Koillismaassa in 1993 under the title Storm Over the Land. The novel depicts the life of Kauko Sammalsuo and his neighbors in a remote area of northeastern Finland during the years 1939-44. P‰‰talo's point of view is on the common soldiers and the families behind the battle fronts. Also Selkosen kansaa (1962) has been translated into English (but not published).

The novels Viimeinen savotta (1966) and Kairank‰vij‰ (1968) inspired the director Edvin Laine, whose Viimeinen savotta was the most successful film in 1977. It won Spede Pasasen's H‰py Endkˆ?, an Uuno Turhapuro comedy, which later in the 1980s were the unbeateble box office hits. Laine's Ruskan j‰lkeen (1979) was based on Mustan lumen talvi (1969) but this time Spede's comedy Koeputkiaikuinen ja Simon Enkelit won with some 80 000 viewers. Laine's production was not a critical success. The two following P‰‰talo films, Life's Hardy Men (1986) and Lumbercamp Tales (1988), which depicted P‰‰talo's childhood and the end of his youth, were directed by Mikko Niskanen. They were Niskanen's last films, made with deep passion and devotion to P‰‰talo's world.

P‰‰talo's Koillismaa series and Iijoki series draw the meticulous picture of the world of farmers and lumber workers' in northern regions of Finland in social and historical settings. The novels are strongly based on P‰‰talo's own experiences and use skillfully dialect and humour to soften the unembellished view of life. During the slow flow of the story, readers share with P‰‰talo his suffering, his persistence, and his success. The author, generally called 'Kalle', did not hide anything from his life – including contracting gonorrhea. Due to his openess and full and detailed narrative, he becomes thoroughfully familiar person, as real as a member of the family .

Most probably Iijoki series, which contains 26 volumes, is the longest narrative autobiograph ever written. The first part, Huonemiehen poika appeared in 1971. P‰‰talo's original plan was not to devote quarter of a century for the work, but his insistence on details stretched the story to giant measures. (In comparison, Michelangelo's work upon the Sistine Chapel took only four years, and Marcel Proust spent the last fourteen years of his life writing Remembrance of Things Past; a work which P‰‰talo most likely never read.) P‰‰talo's exceptional ability to recall with precision events and conversations that are decades old was an unique gift, but it became also a burden – for a truly complete description nothing could be omitted. However, his memory also provided him with a large vocabulary and an extremely keen ear for local language dialect and nuances.

P‰‰talo's path from the backwoods to the position of a celebrated writer had much similarities with the story of Martin Eden, Jack London's hero from his famous novel, or Ivar Lo-Johansson's eight-volume autobiographical series published between 1951 and 1960. P‰‰talo had read an article about the American author before the war and he was especially impressed how persistently London's protagonist struggled to become a writer.

In his own life and his literary works P‰‰talo emphasized the value of honesty and hard work. He served as a model of unyielding struggle against drawbacks of life. Although P‰‰talo has been readers' favorite, the critics have been more reserved. However, in the 1980s and 1990s the author won his decades long war of attrition with the culture establishmed. Among the several awards and homages P‰‰talo eventually received was V‰inˆ Linna Literature Award (1999). Taivalkoski county with Kalle P‰‰talo Association and the P‰‰talo Institute have arranged yearly in July P‰‰talo festivals – the author himself participated at the events from morning to night. P‰‰talo died at the age of 81 in Tampere, on November 20, 2000. He had suffered from the 1990s from pulmonary cancer, probably caused by asbestos which was widely used at construction sites in the 1950s. P‰‰talo's last book, Selkosten viljastaja, was a collection of short stories, written in the 1960s for the magazine Rakennustaito. Posthumously have appeared Kannaksen lomajuna (2001), a collection of stories, and Vikke Nilon tarina (2002), which presents the whole story of one of P‰‰talo's most interesting fictional characters, the colorful lumberjack Vikke Nilo.
